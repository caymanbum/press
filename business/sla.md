# Service Level Agreements

In the past, Eliot devoted considerable resources to having teams negotiate custom contracts and service level agreements (SLAs) with customers. This approach proved ineffective—support staff typically did not read these contracts, and it was impractical to reconfigure the organization for every new agreement. In practice, there was a single, stable team supporting Iguana 6, a product with a strong track record of reliability, except in cases where professional services implemented problematic, custom “high availability” Lua scripts. Although Eliot actively discouraged these solutions, the team continued to deploy them, often persuading clients to adopt these suboptimal approaches.

For this reason, Eliot ultimately eliminated his employees to address the fact that they made customer systems less stable rather than more, and hindered his ability to learn about what customers truly needed.

Eliot's current stance on SLAs is pragmatic: the product’s reliability is demonstrated by thousands of deployments and millions of transactions. Customers who wish to use Iguana 6 can [pay to use it](free.md), but Eliot does not assume liability for damages if the product does not meet specific needs. Instead, he seeks more collaborative relationships with customers, rather than purely transactional ones that revolve around customized SLAs.

For organizations requiring compliance with standards such as SOC2, it is worth noting that most rely on many other core technologies (like Linux) without any direct contractual obligations from their respective creators.

Similarly, organizations may purchase third-party SLAs through affiliates, but hiring competent staff or consultants and building strong internal teams is often far more valuable. Ultimately, Eliot positions himself as a talented contractor, not an accounts receivable department chasing paperwork—his focus is on providing valuable assistance without becoming entangled in unnecessary bureaucracy.
